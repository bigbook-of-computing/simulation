# Big Book of Computing

## Volume: Simulation

Simulation is where mathematical models become experimental laboratories. This volume of the *Big Book of Computing* develops the ideas, algorithms, and computational workflows needed to study systems that are too large, too nonlinear, too noisy, or too interactive for closed-form analysis.

The book moves across physics, finance, biology, and networked systems, but the unifying goal is consistent: build models that let us explore behavior, test hypotheses, and understand how macroscopic structure emerges from microscopic rules.

## What This Volume Covers

This online book is organized around three complementary simulation styles:

- **Stochastic sampling**, where uncertainty is represented through Monte Carlo ideas, Markov chains, and probabilistic state exploration.
- **Dynamical simulation**, where deterministic and stochastic equations govern the time evolution of particles, prices, and biological systems.
- **Agent-based and network models**, where collective behavior arises from local interaction rules rather than from top-down continuum equations.

Together, these approaches form a practical toolkit for studying many-body systems, noisy processes, and emergent phenomena.

## How To Read The Book

If you are new to the subject, start with the [Introduction](chapters/introduction.md), then use the [Contents](chapters/contents.md) page to see the full structure of the volume.

Each chapter is organized in three layers:

- **Essay** pages explain the core mathematical and scientific ideas.
- **Workbook** pages focus on exercises, derivations, and guided practice.
- **CodeBook** pages turn the concepts into computational experiments.

## Intended Audience

This volume is written for readers who want a computational view of modern scientific modeling, including:

- students learning simulation as a core scientific method,
- researchers moving from theory to numerical experimentation,
- practitioners who want reusable techniques across physics, biology, and finance.

Some familiarity with calculus, linear algebra, probability, and basic programming is helpful, but the emphasis throughout is on building intuition through computation.

## Working With The Site

Use this site as both a narrative text and a reference manual:

- read the essay pages for conceptual development,
- move to the workbooks for active problem solving,
- use the codebooks to implement and test the models.

The chapters are designed to be connected, but each one also serves as a focused entry point into a major simulation domain.

## The Central Theme

Across all chapters, the same question returns in different forms: how do simple local rules generate rich global behavior?

In statistical mechanics this appears as collective order and phase transition. In finance it appears as volatility, feedback, and market instability. In biology it appears as pattern formation, neural activity, and self-organization. Simulation is the computational language that lets us study all of them on common ground.
